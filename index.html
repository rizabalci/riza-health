How to fit tennis into your program
Best day: Wednesday (recovery day)
Tennis replaces or supplements your active recovery. Moderate intensity rally sessions and coach drills fit perfectly here. High-intensity match play on Wednesday is fine too — no gym conflict.
Friday or Saturday: second session
Friday is already optional in your program. A second tennis session adds volume without touching gym recovery. Keep Saturday as full rest unless the session is very light.
⚠️ Avoid: day before heavy gym sessions
Don't play intense tennis the evening before push day (Sunday) or pull day (Monday). Shoulder and forearm fatigue directly reduces bench press and overhead press. If you do, drop weight 10-15% and skip overhead press if shoulder feels off.
Calories on tennis days
Add 150-250 kcal depending on intensity. Prioritize carbs pre-session (banana, rice) and protein post-session. Tennis burns ~400-600 kcal/hour at moderate intensity at 95kg.

Padel vs Tennis for your program
Padel court is 10×20m (vs 23×11m for singles tennis) — shorter sprints, more lateral movement, less running. Uses a solid paddle racket, not strings. Walls are in play. Much easier to learn than tennis. Great for active recovery — lower intensity than match tennis but more engaging than swimming.
Typical price per person: €4-8/h when splitting a court 4 ways.
Injury prevention — tennis + lifting
Shoulder: Face pulls (already in your pull day) are the best rotator cuff prevention. Warm up with band rotations before both tennis and push day.
Tennis elbow: Forearm stretches after every session. If you feel lateral elbow pain, reduce grip pressure and see a physio early — do not ignore it.
Calf and ankle: Calf raises (already in lower day) help. Always warm up 5 min before playing — lateral movements without warm-up are the #1 cause of ankle injuries.
If you play an intense match the night before push day, reduce bench press weight 10-15% and skip overhead press if shoulder feels fatigued. Never push through shoulder pain.

How swimming fits your program
Recovery swim (Wednesday): easy freestyle 30-45 min, heart rate below 130. No sprinting. This is active recovery, not training. The goal is blood flow to sore muscles, not cardio.
Calorie burn: at 95kg and moderate pace you burn ~500-600 kcal/hour. Add 200-300 kcal on swim days if hungry.
Sauna protocol: 3 rounds of 10-12 min sauna + cold shower between rounds. Improves recovery, reduces cortisol, evidence for testosterone support. Hydrate 500ml before entering.
Avoid heavy swimming the day before push or pull day. Shoulder fatigue from freestyle affects bench press. Keep pre-gym-day swims easy, 20-30 min max.

Evidence-based protocol
The 3-round protocol (optimal)
Used in Finnish longevity studies and heat shock protein research. Each round: 10-15 min sauna at 80-100°C, followed by cold shower or plunge (30-90 sec), then 5-10 min rest. Repeat 3 times. Total time: 60-80 min.
Round 1 is warmup. Round 2 is where the stress response peaks. Round 3 is where growth hormone release is maximised. Skipping round 3 leaves the best benefits on the table.
Timing relative to training
Best: 6+ hours after training, or on a rest day. The heat blunts the acute inflammatory response needed for muscle adaptation if done immediately post-workout. Wednesday sauna after swimming is ideal.
Do NOT sauna within 2 hours before training. Core temperature elevation reduces strength output significantly.
Testosterone and hormones
2-3 sauna sessions per week shown to increase testosterone by 16% in Finnish studies. Mechanism: heat stress + cold contrast triggers luteinizing hormone release. Cold exposure (cold shower/plunge) amplifies the effect vs sauna alone.
Hydration protocol
Drink 500ml water 30 min before entering. Bring 500ml water for during. Drink another 500ml after. You lose 300-500ml sweat per 15-min round. Dehydration reduces the benefit and causes headaches.
No alcohol before sauna. Alcohol + heat causes rapid blood pressure drop and is genuinely dangerous.

Why steps matter at your goal
8,000 steps burns ~300 kcal at your weight. Over a week that is 2,100 kcal — equivalent to 0.3 kg of fat lost just from walking, without any other change.
Walking is non-fatiguing. Unlike cardio sessions, daily steps don't impair your gym recovery. You can walk after every lift session without affecting next-day performance.
Best practice: walk after every meal (10-15 min). Improves glucose handling, reduces insulin spikes, supports body composition. Especially after dinner.
If you sit at your desk all day (11am-7pm Inspiratek work), you can easily clock under 4,000 steps without trying. Hit your goal before lunch by walking to the gym + a 20-min stretch around noon.

Box Breathing
⏰ Morning · 5 min
Start here. 4 counts in → 4 hold → 4 out → 4 hold. Activates your parasympathetic nervous system within 2 minutes. Used by Navy SEALs and surgeons for acute stress control. Your cortisol spike drops noticeably after 4 rounds.
How to do it:
1. Sit upright, feet flat on floor, hands on knees.
2. Exhale everything out completely.
3. Inhale slowly through nose — count 1, 2, 3, 4.
4. Hold — count 1, 2, 3, 4.
5. Exhale slowly through mouth — count 1, 2, 3, 4.
6. Hold empty — count 1, 2, 3, 4.
7. Repeat 5–8 rounds. Eyes closed. No rushing.
✓ Best done: morning before coffee · Also: before a stressful work call

Breath Focus
⏰ Morning · 5–10 min
Pure attention training. No counting, no technique — just anchor your attention to the physical sensation of breathing and gently return every time it wanders. Each return builds mental focus like a rep builds muscle. Best starting practice for beginners.
How to do it:
1. Sit upright. Spine straight but not stiff. Hands resting on legs.
2. Close your eyes. Take one deep breath and let it go.
3. Breathe naturally — don't control it.
4. Focus only on the sensation: air at nostrils, chest rising, belly expanding.
5. When thoughts appear (they will, constantly), simply notice and return to breath.
6. No judgment. Wandering is not failure — returning is the practice.
7. Set your timer and sit for the full duration.
✓ Best done: morning before coffee · Builds focus and attention over weeks

Body Scan
⏰ Post-gym · 10–15 min
The best post-training meditation. Lying down, you move attention slowly through the body from feet to head. Triggers deep parasympathetic activation, reduces post-training cortisol, and accelerates muscle recovery. Do this directly after your cooldown stretch — not instead of it.
How to do it:
1. Lie flat on your back — on your gym mat, on the floor, or on your bed.
2. Close your eyes. Take 3 slow deep breaths.
3. Bring attention to the soles of your feet. Notice any sensations — warmth, tingling, pressure, nothing.
4. Slowly move attention upward: feet → calves → knees → thighs → hips.
5. Continue: lower back → abdomen → chest → shoulders → arms → hands.
6. Finish: neck → jaw (relax it) → face → top of head.
7. Spend 30–60 sec on each area. If you find tension, breathe into it and let it go.
8. End by taking 3 deep breaths and slowly opening your eyes.
✓ Best done: after gym cooldown, or when you feel physically tense

4-7-8 Breathing
⏰ Pre-sleep · 5 min
The most powerful breathing technique for sleep onset. The extended 8-count exhale activates the vagus nerve, drops heart rate rapidly, and floods the body with calming signals. 4–6 cycles is usually enough to feel significantly calmer. Use this in bed every night.
How to do it:
1. Lie in bed. Lights off. Phone face down.
2. Place tip of tongue behind your upper front teeth (stays there throughout).
3. Exhale completely through your mouth with a whoosh sound.
4. Close mouth. Inhale quietly through nose — count to 4.
5. Hold your breath — count to 7.
6. Exhale completely through mouth with whoosh sound — count to 8.
7. That is one cycle. Repeat 4–6 times.
8. If you fall asleep during — perfect. That is the goal.
✓ Best done: in bed before sleep · Also: when waking at 3am · When anxious or stressed
⚠️ Counts feel long at first — slow them down even further if 7-count hold is uncomfortable. Build up over 2 weeks.

Wim Hof Breathing
⏰ Pre-workout · 10 min
Not traditional meditation but the most energizing breathwork available. 30 deep power breaths → hold on empty → breathe in and hold. Alkalises blood, floods cells with oxygen, releases adrenaline. Can fully replace pre-workout caffeine. Evidence for reduced cortisol, improved cold tolerance, immune function.
How to do it:
1. Sit or lie down. Never do this standing or driving.
2. Take 30 deep, powerful breaths: inhale fully through nose or mouth, exhale let it go (no force on exhale).
3. After 30th breath: exhale and stop. Hold on empty lungs. Hold as long as comfortable.
4. When you need to breathe: inhale fully and hold for 15 seconds.
5. Exhale. That is round 1. Repeat 3 rounds total.
6. Tingling, light-headedness, and warmth are normal. Fainting is possible — always sit or lie down.
✓ Best done: before morning gym session · Replaces pre-workout on off-caffeine days
⚠️ Do not do in water, driving, or standing. Skip if pregnant or epilepsy history.

NSDR / Yoga Nidra
⏰ Afternoon · 10–20 min
Non-Sleep Deep Rest — developed at Stanford by Andrew Huberman. Lying down, guided into a state between sleep and wake. Measurably restores dopamine, reduces cortisol, and improves alertness for 2–3 hours after. Used by Google, elite athletes, and the military. Better than a nap for daytime recovery.
How to do it:
1. Lie on your back — bed, sofa, or floor. Eyes closed.
2. Press play on a guided NSDR or Yoga Nidra track (see links below).
3. Follow the voice completely. Do not try to fall asleep but do not resist if you do.
4. The guide will move your attention through the body, breath, and visualisation.
5. When it ends you will feel rested but not groggy — unlike a nap.
6. Best slot: 13:00–15:00 post-lunch energy dip, or after a poor night's sleep.
✓ Best done: 13:00–15:00 afternoon · After bad sleep · Between work blocks

Visualisation
⏰ Morning or pre-gym · 5 min
Used by elite athletes. You mentally rehearse the training session, the physical sensations, and the successful outcome. Activates the same neural pathways as physical practice. Increases motivation, reduces pre-session anxiety, and improves focus during the actual session.
How to do it:
1. Sit quietly. Eyes closed. 3 slow breaths.
2. Visualise walking into John Reed Schwedenplatz. The smell, the lights, the gym floor.
3. See yourself warming up, loading the barbell, performing your first compound lift.
4. Feel the weight, the bar in your hands, the muscle contracting. Make it vivid.
5. Visualise completing every set cleanly. See your body — leaner, stronger than today.
6. End by seeing yourself finishing the session satisfied. Open eyes and go train.
✓ Best done: 10–15 min before leaving for gym · Or during morning routine

Why this matters for your specific goals
Cortisol is your main enemy. Consulting work, the move, financial pressure, hard training — all spike cortisol. Elevated cortisol suppresses testosterone, increases belly fat storage around organs, and slows muscle protein synthesis. 10 min daily meditation measurably lowers cortisol within 2 weeks.
Sleep: 4-7-8 before bed increases deep sleep and REM duration. Both are when growth hormone and testosterone peak. Poor sleep is the fastest way to undo your training.
Testosterone: meditation increases testosterone by reducing cortisol (cortisol and testosterone are inversely related). Stack this with Ashwagandha and your morning D3+K2 and you have three simultaneous testosterone support tools.
